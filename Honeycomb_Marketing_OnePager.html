The Honeycomb

A half-day succession session families actually want to sit through.

New tool

Succession rarely fails because the family doesn't care. It stalls because no one can make the conversation safe enough to have.

Handovers get delayed, kept vague, or talked around. Founders hold on. The next generation waits for permission. Everyone protects something different, and the direct conversation feels too close to mortality, money, and who is trusted to lead.

57%
of next-generation family members say the current generation's reluctance to retire poses a particular challenge.
Source: PwC Global NextGen Survey 2022.

Why the usual conversation gets stuck

Advice closes people down. Direct questions feel like accusations. Workshops stay abstract and end with a good feeling but nothing written down. The family needs a structure that lowers the emotional risk, puts the hard parts at arm's length, and still produces something they can act on.

How The Honeycomb works

1
Build the boardThe family fills a honeycomb with real succession scenarios — placed at arm's length, on cards, not at each other.
2
Watch the dialsFour shared dials — profitability, harmony, continuity, readiness — move with every choice, making trade-offs visible.
3
Weather the chanceChance cards throw in the events no family chooses, so the plan has to survive the unexpected.
4
Leave with a mapThe finished board becomes a written transition roadmap and one agreed next move.

Cooperative by design: the family plays against the scenario, not each other. No winners, no losers, no decision forced in the room.

What your clients walk out with

  • A visual map of their succession situation.
  • A transition roadmap they wrote themselves.
  • A clear reading on four dimensions of the handover.
  • One concrete next conversation, owned.

Who it's for

  • Coaches and family-business consultants.
  • Advisers running succession or governance work.
  • Educators teaching family enterprise.

Built on the Succession Dynamics deck — 60 hexagonal scenario cards — so it sits straight into a trainer's toolbox.
