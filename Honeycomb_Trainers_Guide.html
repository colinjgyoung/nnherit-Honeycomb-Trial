The Honeycomb

A facilitated, half-day succession session built on the Succession Dynamics deck. Working title — mechanics for the first test run.

What this is. A guide for the coach, consultant or adviser running The Honeycomb. It covers what the session is for, what's in the kit, the rules and scoring, and a minute-by-minute flow. The Honeycomb is a new tool concept, not a finished product — treat this as the script for a test run, not a fixed rulebook.

What it's for

Family businesses rarely struggle for want of a succession framework. They struggle to make the handover discussable — to sequence it, name the risks, and talk about founder control, readiness and timing without the conversation turning personal. The Honeycomb gives a family a shared object to work on instead of each other. Players build a complete honeycomb out of real succession scenarios, watch the effect on four family dials, absorb a few unpredictable events, and leave with a transition roadmap they wrote themselves.

It is cooperative by design. The family plays against the scenario, not against each other. There are no winners, no losers, and no forced decision about who succeeds.

Who runs it, and for whom

  • Facilitator: a coach, consultant, family-business adviser or educator. The tool supports the facilitator — it does not replace facilitation.
  • Participants: a founder plus next generation, a sibling group, or a small family-and-adviser group. Best at 3–7 people.
  • Setting: a half-day working session. A full-day variation is noted at the end.

The four family dials

Each scenario card carries impact scores. As cards go onto the board, those scores add up on four shared dials. The family's shared goal is a completed honeycomb with all four dials above the agreed line.

ProfitabilityCan the business keep performing through the handover?
HarmonyAre family relationships holding?
ContinuityIs the enterprise set up to carry forward?
ReadinessIs the next generation ready to lead?

Each card shows a difficulty rating from 1 to 4 (a slider on the back of the card). Card colour identifies the challenge — one colour for each of the five challenges — it does not encode difficulty. Difficulty is about how complex a situation is, not whether it is good or bad.

Core rules

  1. Build outward from the Legacy Core. Place scenario cards into empty hexes, working out from the centre.
  2. Talk before you place. A card only goes down once the group has said why it belongs there and what it would mean for the family.
  3. Watch the dials. Every placement moves the four shared dials by the card's own impact scores — problem scenarios pull them down, constructive ones lift them. This comes from the scores, not the card's colour.
  4. Weather the chance cards. At set points, draw a chance card. It swings a dial and the family adapts — replacing, re-sequencing, or strengthening their plan.
  5. Finish the shape. The session resolves when the honeycomb is complete and the family reads where the dials have landed.
The point of the dials is the conversation, not the score. A number going down is a prompt to ask "what would protect this?" — not a failure. Keep the family's attention on what each placement reveals.

Session flow — half day (about 3 hours 30)

PhaseTimeWhat happens
1. Welcome & ground rules20 minSet the contract: this is cooperative, nothing leaves the room, no decision is forced today. Agree how the family will flag if something gets too close.
2. Frame the board15 minIntroduce the Legacy Core, the four dials, and what "good" looks like on each. Let the family set the line they want to clear.
3. Surface the challenges40 minWorking from the deck, the family places the scenarios that feel true to their situation. Reds and oranges will pull the dials down — that's expected. Name what each one really is.
4. First chance round20 minDraw one or two chance cards. The family sees what an external shock does to a fragile plan, and adapts.
5. Build the resolutions45 minNow place the moves that recover the dials — phased transitions, clearer mandates, open conversations. The honeycomb fills outward.
6. Complete the shape20 minFill the final hexes. Read where the four dials have settled. If one is still at risk, the family chooses one more move.
7. Roadmap & debrief40 minWrite the finished board up as a transition roadmap, in board order. Debrief: what surprised us, what we avoided naming before, what is now sayable.
8. Close & next move15 minAgree the single next conversation or action. End on something concrete and owned.

The chance mechanic

Chance cards add luck and a little tension, and they do real facilitation work: they show a family that a succession plan has to survive events nobody chose. Examples in the test deck: Market shock, Health event, Rival bid, Key person leaves, Family rift, and a few positive turns such as Unexpected windfall and Mentor steps in. Each swings one or more dials. The facilitator decides when to draw — usually once the board feels settled and a little complacent.

Facilitation principles

  • Keep it cooperative. If anyone starts keeping personal score, bring the focus back to the shared dials and the board.
  • Protect the founder. Founder-reluctance cards touch identity and control. Use the card and the scenario as the distance — discuss "this founder," then let the room bring it closer only if it's ready.
  • Pace the exposure. You set the speed. Don't let the group surface more than it can hold in the time available.
  • Make the output visible. A session that ends in "good conversation" but no written roadmap has under-delivered. The capture sheet is not optional.
  • Debrief is where the learning lands. Leave real time for it. The board is the prompt; the debrief is the value.

What the family leaves with

  • A completed honeycomb — a visual map of their succession situation.
  • A written transition roadmap, in the order they worked through it.
  • A reading on four dimensions of where the handover currently stands.
  • One agreed next move.

Full-day variation

For a full day, run two honeycombs — one for the problem as it stands, one for the family's preferred path — with a longer chance sequence between them, and a deeper debrief. This is also the natural bridge into the wider Legacy Lab journey, where succession is one territory among ownership, governance and renewal.
